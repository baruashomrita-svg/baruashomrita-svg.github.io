About

A clinician turned policy-minded researcher.

My path moved from chairside care in Bangladesh to community health in Connecticut — but the question stayed the same: how do we close the gap between the care people need and the care they actually receive?

I trained as a dentist (BDS, University of Chittagong) and earned my first MPH at the National Institute of Preventive and Social Medicine in Bangladesh, where I supported the national COVID-19 response, telehealth, and policy reporting at the Directorate General of Health Services. Those years taught me how policy decisions ripple through frontline care — especially for marginalized communities.

At the University of Connecticut (MPH, 2026), I have focused that lens on health equity in the United States. My practicum at the Connecticut Department of Public Health centered on refugee and immigrant health, and my current internship advances language access for Limited English Proficient (LEP) populations under federal civil rights frameworks (Title VI, EO 13166). My capstone examines mental health service utilization among South Asian immigrants — a community I belong to and a topic where the gap between need and access is striking.

Across this work, I bring three habits: community-based listening (I learn from people, not just literature), policy literacy (I read the law and ask what it means in clinic), and visual translation (I turn evidence into infographics, posters, and tools that communities and decision-makers actually use).

MPH Capstone · 2025–2026

Mental Health Service Utilization Among South Asian Immigrants in the U.S.

An evidence synthesis examining cultural, structural, and policy-level barriers to mental health care for South Asian immigrants — and what culturally responsive systems could look like.

Health Equity Mental Health Immigrant Health Cultural Competence Language Access Policy Analysis

Why this question matters

South Asian Americans are one of the fastest-growing immigrant communities in the U.S., yet remain among the lowest utilizers of formal mental health services. National data and community studies point to a pattern: real psychological distress is met with stigma, language barriers, and a workforce that rarely reflects clients' cultural frames of meaning. Behind those individual experiences sit policy and system choices — what gets reimbursed, who gets credentialed, and which languages a clinic serves.

Approach

  • Mixed-methods synthesis of peer-reviewed literature and grey literature on South Asian immigrant mental health utilization in the U.S.
  • Analysis of barriers organized at three levels — individual/cultural, provider/clinical, and structural/policy.
  • Review of language access policy (Title VI of the Civil Rights Act, Executive Order 13166, CMS LEP guidance) and its on-the-ground implementation gaps.
  • Mapping of culturally responsive service delivery models with implications for community-based organizations and state Departments of Public Health.

Key themes emerging

  • Stigma as a public-health concern — not a private one — that systems can either reinforce or actively dismantle through community partnership.
  • Language access remains the single most modifiable structural barrier; policy exists, but enforcement and provider-level practice lag.
  • Cultural competence is not optional training: it shapes whether clients return after a first visit.
  • Trust is built upstream — through schools, faith institutions, and primary care — long before a mental health referral.

Policy & practice implications I am developing

The capstone closes with recommendations targeted at three audiences: state health agencies (language access enforcement, workforce diversity pipelines), community-based organizations (peer-led models, faith-based partnerships, multilingual psychoeducation), and clinical training programs (cultural humility curricula that move beyond a single lecture). Language Access Policy

Implementation Gaps in Title VI & EO 13166 for LEP Patient Populations

An analytic review of how federal language access requirements translate (or fail to translate) into clinical practice for Limited English Proficient (LEP) patients. Examines the divergence between policy on paper and the lived experience at the registration desk, in the exam room, and in mental health settings — where qualified interpreters are still inconsistently available.

Frameworks reviewed: Title VI of the Civil Rights Act of 1964 · Executive Order 13166 · CMS LEP guidance · HHS Office for Civil Rights enforcement actions.
Refugee & Immigrant Health

Comparative Cross-Jurisdictional Analysis: State DPH Refugee Health Programs

Conducted at the Connecticut Department of Public Health, this analysis compares refugee health screening, follow-up, and referral practices across multiple state DPH programs to surface best practices, equity gaps, and policy recommendations. Included key informant interviews and synthesis of program data into actionable findings for interagency partners.

Methods: cross-site comparison · stakeholder interviews · qualitative coding · systems mapping.
